Case File

The Missing Vermeer Painting

Stolen Artwork Investigation

Location: Private Study Room (Penthouse Level)

Access: Single Entry Point (Hallway Connection)

Window: Floor-to-Ceiling Glass, Facing City Skyline

Lighting: Low Ambient Illumination

Condition: Frame intact, canvas removed

Case Overview

A recently rediscovered painting attributed to Johannes Vermeer, valued at approximately $80 million, was reported stolen during a private black-tie gala held in a penthouse gallery. The incident occurred within a secluded study room used to display the piece. Upon discovery, the painting's frame remained mounted on the wall; however, the canvas had been carefully and precisely removed, indicating a deliberate and controlled extraction of the piece.

Protection & Display Specifications

Appraised Value: $80,000,000 USD (verified, Harwick & Solen Fine Art Insurers, Ref. #HS-2024-441)
Authentication Status: Conditionally verified. A secondary review was submitted by an unnamed party prior to the event citing potential attribution concerns. Review was pending at time of theft.

Frame: Original period gilded oak, appraised separately at $340,000. Mounting hardware reinforced with tamper-resistant steel anchor bolts rated to 400kg lateral force. Frame remained fully intact post-incident.

Security System: Motion detection (Level 3 sensitivity) active within the study. Alarm logs show a triggered event at 21:58 — attributed to guest proximity. No secondary trigger was recorded. System remained armed throughout the evening.

Display Surface: Canvas housed behind proprietary nano-structured glazing (Product Ref: NE-7 "Moth Eye" Nano-Structured Glass).

Environmental Controls: Humidity sensor active. No anomalies recorded. Temperature within the room was not monitored independently of the broader venue system.

Scene of the Crime

The study is spatially confined and lacks adequate ventilation. During peak hours of the event, the number of guests increased noticeably, particularly between 9:50 PM and 10:05 PM, resulting in elevated temperature and reduced personal space within the room. Visibility conditions remained limited due to intentionally subdued lighting designed to emphasize the artwork.

Evidence Summary

No signs of forced entry were observed at the access point or surrounding structures. The protective glass remained intact and undisturbed. The canvas appears to have been removed using a precise cutting method, suggesting familiarity with both the artwork's construction and the display setup. The absence of immediate disturbance indicates the act was carried out efficiently.
